Cereals & Pulses

Description:

Cereal, also called grain, composed of the endosperm, germ and bran. Cereal crops are grown in greater quantities and provide more food energy worldwide than any other type of crop and are therefore staple crops. In their unprocessed whole grain form, cereals are a rich source of vitamins, minerals, carbohydrates, fats, oils and protein. When processed by the removal of the bran and germ the remaining endosperm is mostly carbohydrate. The cereals most commonly cultivated are: Wheat, Rice, Rye, Oats, Barley, Corn (Maize) and Sorghum.

A legume when used as a dry grain, the seed is also called a pulse. Pulses are grown agriculturally, primarily for human consumption, for livestock forage and silage, and as soil- enriching green manure. Pulse crops are among the best plant based sources of protein, dietary fiber, other nutrients such as iron and calcium. Pulses are used as a key ingredient in vegan meat and dairy substitutes. Well known pulses include beans, soybeans, chickpea, lentils, alfalfa etc.

Wheat

Wheat is world’s most widely cultivated crop. The starch from wheat is used to improve the strength of paper, gluten is used in the pharmaceuticals industry to create capsules and germ, which contains lots of vitamin E, is commonly used in soaps and creams. Wheat is typically milled into flour which is then used to make a wide range of foods including bread, crumpets, muffins, noodles, pasta, biscuits, cakes, pastries, cereal bars, sweet and savoury snack foods, crackers, crisp-breads, sauces and confectionery (e.g. liquorice).

Millets:

The year 2023 has been declared as “International Year of the Millets” by United Nations. Millets have a low Glycaemic Index (GI), good source of minerals like iron, zinc, and calcium and also associated with the prevention of diabetes. Millets are gluten-free and can be consumed by celiac disease patients and help to reduce weight, BMI, and high blood pressure. Millet based value-added products in ready to cook, ready to eat category are easily accessible and convenient to the urban population. Millet cultivation helps to reduce the carbon footprint in the soil.

Pulses or Legumes :

Staples dishes and cuisines from across the world feature pulses, from hummus in the Mediterranean (chick peas), to a traditional full English breakfast (baked navy beans) to Indian dal (peas or lentils).

Pulses provide protein as well as a significant source of vitamins and minerals, such as iron, zinc and magnesium. In addition, pulses are high in fiber and have a low glycemic index, making them particularly beneficial to people with diabetes by assisting in maintaining healthy blood glucose and insulin levels. The phytochemicals, saponins, and tannins found in pulses possess antioxidant and anti-carcinogenic effects, indicating that pulses may have significant anti-cancer effects. Pulse consumption also improves serum lipid profiles and lower the risk factors, such as blood pressure, platelet activity, and inflammation.
